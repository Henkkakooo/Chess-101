Master the terminology first before checking the tips!

Here's all the necessary terminology for a beginner

  • Absolute pin

    The piece that's "pinned" is in front of the king, making it (the piece in front) illegal to move

  • Back rank

    The players first rank (where the king is positioned)

  • Blunder

    Making a bad move that results in loss of material

  • Capture

    Capturing a piece

  • Checkmate

    Checkmate is given when a player’s king is threatened with capture and there’s no way to evade the capture

  • File

    A vertical column on the chess board

  • Fork

    A simultaneous attack on two or more of the oppontents pieces resulting in material gain.

  • Castling

    King moves two spaces to it's right/left - rook moves to the opposite side of the king

  • Major pieces

    Queen and Rooks

  • Minor Pieces

    Knights and Bishops

  • Pin

    Pin is where a player has one or more pieces in front of a (valuable) piece making it vunerable to a capture if moved

  • Promoting a Pawn

    Moving the pawn to the opponents back rank makes it possible to promote the pawn into any other piece for example, a queen

  • Stalemate

    Stalemate is a situation in which the opposing player has no legal moves left - results in a draw

Here you'll find useful tips & tricks to implement on your games during the opening, middle game and the end game phase.

Opening Tips

  • Defend your pieces

    It's easy to get carried away in a game of Chess, but the most important thing to remember when moving a piece is to check if every piece has been defended by some other piece. Especially the most valuable pieces (rook, queen).

  • Develop minor pieces

    Minor pieces (knigths & bishops) should be developed first before bringing out the queen. You have to be careful not to block any minor pieces with pawn moves and not to stay behind development by moving too many pawns.

  • Making a good move

    Before making the next move, you should always look for checks, captures and attacks. Then evaluate - is the possible check, capture or attack a good one? If one of these three steps lead to a better position than you had before, then go for it. If not, then defend and fortify your position.

  • Move each piece only once

    By moving each piece only once, you'll be able to develop your pieces more efficiently. This means not getting carried away with chasing the opponents piece with many moves with the same piece for example, attacking the opponents queen with many knight moves. Usually making too many repetitive moves will result in a lack of development and a worse position.

Middle Game tips

  • Notice unguarded squares

    Notice the unguarded squares and defend them against the opponents attacks. This will result in a harder to attack position for the opponent.

  • Utilize the Rooks

    It's easy to forget and when you do, it's usually too late. Try to bring out your rooks to the party after castling and having a solid position. Rooks will provide reinforcements and help closing files.

  • Strategize with consideration

    It's true that strategies come up naturally during the game if one is observant enough. But it's also important to analyze the strategy your going with. Visualize the moves, possible countermoves and make sure it's profitable before going through with your plan.

End Game tips

  • Hold on to past pawns

    Past pawns are the key to success in the end game. Supporting the past pawn's way to promotion will lead to an easy checkmate.

  • Be vary of forks

    When moving your pieces in the engame, check for possible forks before deciding the move. It's common to loose track of your last pieces and "walk into" a fork. Take a breather, analyze and move your piece to a safe square.

  • Stalemate < Checkmate

    Having an upper hand in the end game can be exhilarating but it's exactly the time to focus the most. Make sure that the move you're making leads to a check, checkmate OR the opponents king being able to move after. In some games, the opponent's last resort to not loose is to sacrifice a piece with a stalemate in mind. You'll avoid any unnecessary stalemates by making sure that the opponents king has a square to move to unless you have a check / checkmate.
